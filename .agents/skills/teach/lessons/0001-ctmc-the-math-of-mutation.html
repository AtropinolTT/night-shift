🧬 CTMC: The Math of Mutation

How a Continuous-Time Markov Chain lets us model amino acid evolution — no scary formulas, just intuition.

1. Start With a Real Mutation

Look at this real edge from our BCR tree data:

Parent: CASSLGTSGGYEQYF
Child: CASSLGTGGGYEQYF

At position 9 (counting from 0), a mutation happened: S → G (Serine → Glycine).

A few questions the model needs to answer:

  • How likely is an S→G mutation vs S→A vs S→P?
  • Does the answer depend on where in the sequence we are (CDR vs framework)?
  • If we wait twice as long, does the mutation chance double? (Spoiler: no — it's an exponential approach, not linear)

To answer these rigorously, we need the CTMC framework. Let's build it from the ground up.

2. Intuition First: The Rate Concept

Imagine you're watching a single amino acid position in a BCR sequence over time.

🏠 You're in state S (Serine)

At any moment, you could mutate to another amino acid. Some transitions are more likely than others.

⏱️ The "rate" is a tendency

A rate of R[S→G] = 2.0 means "in a very short time dt, you're about 2×dt likely to jump to G."

🧠 Memoryless property

The rate right now doesn't depend on how long you've been in state S. Freshness doesn't change mutation probability.

🎲 Competing risks

You can jump to any of 19 other AAs. Whichever happens first wins. Total exit rate = sum of all 19 rates from S.

🍕 Analogy: You're at a party (state S). You could leave to go to the pizza place (G), the taco truck (A), or home (stop codon). Each option has a "rate" — how tempted you are per minute. The higher the rate, the sooner you'll make that move on average. But the actual time is random, not fixed.

3. The Rate Matrix: The Heart of CTMC

A CTMC is defined by one thing: the rate matrix Q (or in our code, R).

For 20 amino acids, Q is a 20×20 matrix:

Q[i][j] = rate of going i → j (i ≠ j)  ·  Q[i][i] = -Σ Q[i][j] for j≠i

The diagonal is negative — it's the sum of all exit rates. This makes every row sum to zero, which is a mathematical requirement for conservation of probability.

→ A→ C→ D→ E→ G…
From S0.30.020.10.052.0…
From G0.50.010.050.2−……
Each row = "from" amino acid. Each column = "to" amino acid.
S→G has rate 2.0 (high), S→C has rate 0.02 (low — rare mutation).

Our model predicts one 20×20 rate matrix per position in the sequence — because CDR positions mutate differently from framework positions. That's what the Transformer at the core of DiscreteFlowCore does: it looks at the sequence context and outputs the right Q matrix for each position.

4. From Rate to Probability: The Magic of Matrix Exponential

Here's the key question: Given the rate matrix Q, after time t, what's the probability of being at each amino acid?

The answer is the matrix exponential:

P(t) = exp(Q × t)

This is NOT element-wise exp! It's the matrix exponential — the solution to the differential equation dP/dt = P × Q.

🧠 Intuition: Think of P(t) as the "cumulative effect" of all possible paths the mutation could take.

If Q says "S→G at rate 2.0" and "S→A at rate 0.3", then:
  • At t=0: P = identity matrix (you're definitely still S)
  • At small t: P[S→G] ≈ 2.0 × t, P[S→A] ≈ 0.3 × t
  • At large t: P approaches a steady distribution (the equilibrium)
The matrix exponential captures all of this — including the possibility of multiple mutations (S→G→A in two steps).

⏸️ Quick check: Why can't we just use P = I + Q×t (the linear approximation) for large t?

  1. Because probabilities must stay ≤ 1, but linear growth would exceed 1 for large t. The matrix exponential correctly saturates.
  2. Because matrix multiplication is too slow for large t.
  3. Because Q changes over time, so linear approximation doesn't work.
Correct: If P[S→G] ≈ 2.0 × t, then at t=0.6 the probability would be 1.2 (>1.0!) which is impossible. The matrix exponential correctly accounts for the fact that as probability accumulates at G, there's less probability left at S to depart from. It's self-limiting — like compound interest in reverse. This is why the rows of exp(Q×t) always sum to 1.

5. CTMC in the Context of Flow Matching

Now here's the connection to our coupled_loss function:

Training step:

  1. We have a parent and a child sequence from a real tree edge
  2. We sample a random time t uniformly from (0, 1)
  3. We ask: "At this random time, what's the probability of observing the child given the parent, under our predicted rate matrix?"
  4. We maximize this probability (minimize negative log-likelihood)

The model learns: "For this position, in this context, the rate of S→G should be high and the rate of S→C should be low" — exactly the pattern in real SHM.

At generation time, we reverse the process:

  • Start from a parent sequence
  • At each tiny time step Δt, compute the current rate matrix Q
  • Sample mutations according to P = exp(Q × Δt)
  • Step forward in time until t=1
  • The result: a realistic child sequence

6. Wait — Why Flow Matching? Why Not Just Count Mutations?

Good question. If we have 2,765 real tree edges, why not just build a frequency table: "In CDR, S→G happened 45 times, S→A happened 12 times..."?

Three reasons we need CTMC + flow matching instead:
ApproachLimitation
Simple countingCan't generalize to positions or contexts not seen in training data. No notion of continuous time.
Autoregressive LM (e.g., Transformer predicting child from parent)Fixed one-step prediction — can't generate trajectories or intermediate states. No continuous-time control.
Flow matching (our approach) ✅ Continuous time — can simulate evolution at any time scale
✅ Generalizes via learned rate functions, not lookup tables
✅ Guidance mechanism — steer toward antigen binding
✅ Smooth trajectories, not just one big jump

🧪 Scenario: Your model generates a child with 17 mutations, but the real data has 26.5 ± 39.2 mutations. The model produces mostly conservative substitutions (similar amino acids). What's happening?

  1. The model is overfitting to noise in the training data.
  2. This is regression to the mean — the model learns the average mutation count and plays it safe. It's conservative: it produces plausible-looking sequences with low variance, missing the diversity of real SHM.
  3. The model's rate matrix is incorrect and needs to be replaced.
Correct: This is exactly what we observe in our model (17.6 ± 1.8 mutations vs real 26.5 ± 39.2). The model has learned the "average" behavior well — it generates perfect-looking sequences (100% valid) — but it's playing it safe. The variance is too low. This is a known challenge with flow matching: the model converges to the mean path unless specifically encouraged to explore more. It's not broken — but it's not capturing the full distribution yet.

7. From CTMC to the Full Model

The CTMC is just the discrete core. But our model has two more pieces:

🧩 Discrete Core (CTMC)

Transformer → 20×20 rate matrix per position. Models the biochemical likelihood of each mutation.

🧭 Guidance MLP

Predicts velocity in AbLang2 embedding space. Captures functional/antigenic properties.

🔗 Coupling

score = rate × exp(β · cos_sim(v, Δ_embedding))
The guidance score modulates the discrete rate. If β=0, pure CTMC (Mode A).

🌳 Tree Data

2,765 parent→child edges from 281 clones. Each edge = one training example.

The coupling formula is where the magic lives:

score(i → j) = R[i→j] × exp( β · cos_sim(v, e_child - e_parent) )

If β = 0, the guidance term = 1, and we're left with pure CTMC rate → Mode A: unconditional SHM simulation.

If β > 0 and v points toward a target antigen embedding → Mode B: antigen-guided evolution.

8. Vocabulary: What is a "Balanced Tree"?

You asked about balanced trees earlier. Here's the quick answer (we'll go deeper in Lesson 2):

In BCR lineage trees:

  • Star tree: One ancestor, all leaves directly attached. Looks like a starfish. Implies no branching structure — all mutations happened independently. This is usually an artifact of poor tree inference, not real biology.
  • Balanced tree: Branching events are spread out — the tree has intermediate nodes (internal nodes) that represent shared ancestors of subsets of sequences. Matches the biological expectation: B cells divide, mutate, and some daughter cells continue to divide and mutate.

🌳 Our data had 53% star trees before Bug #2 was fixed. What was the bug?

  1. The tree-building algorithm was using the wrong distance metric.
  2. All unnamed internal nodes got the empty string '' as their dictionary key — so every unnamed internal node overwrote the previous one. Result: every tree had exactly 1 internal node → all trees were stars, regardless of the real topology.
  3. The sequences were too short to infer branching structure.
This is a real bug from our project history! Line 46-51 of build_graph_data.py: if node.name and node.name in name_to_idx — the fix was adding the node.name and check. Before the fix: if node.name in name_to_idx → empty string always checks True for '' (since it was added for the first unnamed node), so all subsequent unnamed nodes were treated as the same node. After the fix: unnamed nodes always get unique indices. The mean internal nodes went from 1.0 → 15.1. This is a cautionary tale about trusting your tree data!

Summary: What You Should Remember

  • CTMC = Markov chain with continuous time. Defined by a rate matrix Q (20×20 for AAs).
  • Q[i][j] = instantaneous rate of i→j. Higher = faster / more likely.
  • Matrix exponential P(t) = exp(Q×t) converts rates to probabilities over time t. It's the solution to the ODE dP/dt = P×Q.
  • Training: Sample random t, maximize P(parent → child at time t) under predicted Q.
  • Our model: Transformer predicts one Q per position + guidance MLP modulates rates.
  • β=0 → pure CTMC simulation (Mode A). Works now.
  • Star trees = artifact. Balanced trees = real biology. Always verify tree topology.
